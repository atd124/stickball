How can you not be romantic about baseball stickball?

What do you do when you can't access a baseball field and most of your friends are out of town for the summer? Play stickball.

By Anthony D'Arrigo

August 7, 2022

Stickball is like one-on-one baseball, only the rules, field, and equipment are quirky, random, and subject to change (sometimes as you're playing). Two outs per inning. No walks. No bases. Five, maybe six, innings. Want to play? Concrete wall to substitute the catcher and ump is a must; chalk to outline the strike zone after it rains too. Don't forget your mitt, wood bat, and a few old tennis balls (to dull the bounce off the bat). Home run distance? Depends on the field.

Field of dreams—in Jersey?

On a recent visit to my parents in New Jersey, I thought it would be fun to introduce my boys, 6 and 10, to stickball. They had just discovered backyard wiffle ball (completely different rules) so the time was right to show them where their Dad spent the summer of '92 playing his favorite childhood "sport." Like Clark Griswold conceiving a new family tradition, I built the moment up in my imagination as we walked to the park. Would my sons fall in love with stickball like I did? Would neighborhood kids be playing? Was the strikezone outline still there?

No, no, and no.
